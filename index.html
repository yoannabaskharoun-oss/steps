why tiny works

Big tasks hide the first move

Executive dysfunction rarely means a task is too hard. It usually means the first move is invisible — buried under everything the task quietly implies.

"Clean the kitchen" hides a dozen unspoken decisions: what counts as clean, where to start, how long it'll take. "Put three dishes in the sink" hides none of that. It's just a thing your hands can do right now.

Stepstones does the hiding-work for you — finding the smallest physical action available, and handing you just that. Nothing else, until you're ready for the next stone.

vague "Clean my room" — no clear first move, easy to stall on
tiny "Pick up 5 things off the floor" — done in under 3 minutes
vague "Reply to that email" — feels bigger the longer it waits
tiny "Open it and read just the subject line" — no pressure to finish
